üé∏ Hearing Risk by Instrument

Different instruments present different challenges. Understanding your specific risk helps you choose the right protection.

ü•Å Drums & Percussion

Peak levels: 100-130 dB

Highest risk category. Sudden peaks from cymbals and snare can cause immediate damage.

  • Cymbals: 110-125 dB at ear level
  • Snare rim shots: 115-130 dB
  • Always wear protection, even at practice

üé∏ Electric Guitar/Bass

Typical levels: 90-110 dB

Amp proximity is the main danger. Stage volume wars make it worse.

  • Half-stack at stage volume: 100-110 dB
  • Position matters enormously
  • In-ears eliminate amp dependency

üé∑ Brass & Woodwinds

Typical levels: 85-110 dB

Your own instrument is the main exposure source. Orchestra pits amplify risk.

  • Trumpet bell: 95-110 dB
  • Proximity to other brass matters
  • Pit acoustics can trap sound

üé§ Vocals

Monitor levels: 95-110 dB

Wedge monitors blasting at your face are the primary hazard.

  • Monitor wedges point right at you
  • IEMs transform vocal monitoring
  • Side-fill can be just as dangerous

üéπ Keys & Piano

Varies: 75-100 dB

Lowest direct risk, but stage position often puts you near drums or amps.

  • Grand piano: 85-100 dB
  • Often positioned near drums
  • Monitor exposure is main risk

üéª Strings (Orchestra)

Typical levels: 80-95 dB

Lower than rock, but cumulative hours matter. Brass section proximity is key.

  • Violin at ear: 85-95 dB
  • Sitting near brass: +10-15 dB
  • 3-4 hour rehearsals add up

Stage Volume Reduction Strategies

  • Drum shields: Plexiglass panels reduce drum bleed to other musicians by 10-15 dB
  • Amp tilting: Tilt amps up to point at your ears, not your knees‚Äîyou'll turn down naturally
  • Iso cabs: Isolate guitar amps offstage and mic them‚Äîtotal stage volume control
  • Monitor positioning: Angle wedges to your ears, not your shins. You need less volume.
  • In-ear adoption: Eliminates wedges entirely. Each person controls their own mix.

üè† Practice Room Safety

Most hearing damage happens in practice, not performance. Here's how to protect yourself daily.

1

Acoustic Treatment

Untreated rooms reflect sound back at you, effectively doubling exposure. Basic absorption panels reduce this significantly.

2

Volume Discipline

Practice at the lowest volume that lets you hear what you need. Save full volume for soundcheck and performance only.

3

Break Protocol

Take 5-10 minute quiet breaks every hour. Your ears need recovery time even at moderate volumes.

4

Rehearsal Protection

If you won't wear earplugs in rehearsal, you won't wear them at the gig. Build the habit early.

üéß In-Ear Monitors: The Game Changer

IEMs aren't just about hearing protection‚Äîthey transform your monitoring experience entirely.

Benefits Beyond Hearing Protection

  • Perfect mix control: You hear exactly what you need, at the volume you choose
  • Stage freedom: Move anywhere without losing your monitor mix
  • Consistency: Same mix every night, regardless of venue acoustics
  • Less vocal strain: Singers don't compete with wedges, protect their voice

Getting Started with IEMs

  1. Start with universal fits: $100-300 IEMs let you try the workflow before investing in customs
  2. Get impressions made: Audiologist takes molds of your ear canals (30 min, ~$50)
  3. Choose your drivers: More drivers = more frequency precision, but diminishing returns above 4-6
  4. Consider ambient mics: Blend in crowd/room sound so you don't feel isolated

‚ö†Ô∏è IEM Warning

IEMs seal so well that you can damage your hearing at lower volumes than you'd expect. Use a limiter in your signal chain, and start quieter than you think you need.

The Longevity Protocol

  • Annual hearing tests: Catch small changes before they become big problems
  • No exceptions: Wear protection at every rehearsal, every show, every loud situation
  • 16-hour recovery: After intense exposure, give your ears 16+ hours of quiet
  • Volume budgeting: If you have a show tonight, avoid loud environments during the day
  • Take it seriously: Hearing loss is permanent. There's no coming back from it.
