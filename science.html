Sound is a Wave

Sound travels through air as invisible waves of pressure ‚Äî like ripples in water, but in 3D. These waves have two key properties: how fast they wiggle (frequency) and how big they are (amplitude).

Why This Matters

Frequency determines pitch ‚Äî how high or low a tone sounds. Bass has low frequency; a whistle has high frequency. Amplitude determines intensity and volume ‚Äî bigger waves mean greater intensity. Loudness is our subjective perception of frequency and amplitude combined ‚Äî bigger waves = louder sound = more potential for damage.

Decibels: Measuring Loudness

Decibels (dB) measure sound intensity objectively. The scale isn't linear: sound energy doubles for every 3 dB increase, but perceived loudness doubles at roughly 10 dB. How Hearing Damage Happens

Hearing damage is sneaky. It's usually gradual, painless, and irreversible. The shear force from intense sound bends the tiny hair cells in your cochlea until they detach from the membrane that nourishes them ‚Äî and once they're gone, they're gone forever.

The Optimal Allowable Threshold

At 85 dB (busy traffic, noisy restaurant), you can safely listen for about 8 hours before risking damage. This is the baseline.

Getting Risky

At 94 dB (motorcycle, power tools), safe exposure drops to just 1 hour. Every 3 dB increase cuts safe time in half.

At 110 dB (front row at a concert, loud headphones), damage can occur in just 2 minutes. This is where many music fans live.
